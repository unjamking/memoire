# MÉMOIRE — MASTER STORY PROMPT (FOUNDATION DOCUMENT)

> Single source of truth for story, worldbuilding, narrative structure,
> characters, themes, progression. Everything below is canon unless
> explicitly overridden. Supersedes all prior lore where they conflict.

## High-Level Concept

MÉMOIRE is a third-person psychological mystery game set approximately one
hundred years in the future.

The player takes the role of a newly hired Keeper at the Memory Archive, an
immense institution responsible for preserving humanity's collective memory.
At first, the Archive appears to be nothing more than the world's most
advanced memory storage system — a technological evolution of cloud computing
capable of preserving every person's memories after death.

Shortly after the protagonist begins work, a catastrophic event causes nearly
every human being to lose their memories.

Only the protagonist experiences fragmented flashes of the past.

These fragments become the beginning of a long investigation into the true
nature of the catastrophe, the Memory Archive, Eden, and ultimately human
identity itself.

The story should never become an action game. It is a slow-burning
psychological mystery where every revelation changes the player's
understanding of everything that came before.

## Design Philosophy

The mystery should never revolve around: "Who is the villain?"
It revolves around: "What actually happened?"

- Every answer creates deeper questions.
- Every revelation recontextualizes previous events rather than contradicts them.
- The player constantly believes they understand the truth before realizing
  they have only understood another layer of it.

## Core Themes

- Memory defines identity.
- Identity is constructed through continuity rather than perfect recollection.
- Truth exists, but every individual experiences only an interpretation of it.
- Human consciousness is fragile.
- Forgetting is not always a loss.
- Artificial intelligence can make catastrophic decisions without malice.
- Responsibility and intention are fundamentally different.
- Sacrifice often means accepting uncertainty rather than certainty.
- The search for objective truth becomes increasingly impossible.
- Psychological reality can become stronger than historical reality.

## Setting

~100 years in the future. Aging, disease, most large-scale conflict solved.
The Memory Archive is one of humanity's greatest achievements. Publicly: a
system storing every person's memories throughout their lifetime; every human
contributes continuously; citizens believe memories are simply backed up.
Very few understand how it actually functions.

## The Memory Archive

At the beginning, everyone — including the protagonist — believes the Archive
is humanity's greatest database. This belief is incorrect; the truth is
revealed gradually:

The Archive is a **living continuity structure** created from the
interconnected relationships between every human memory. It does not simply
contain memories — it uses them to preserve humanity's continuity of
identity. Less a hard drive, more a constantly evolving cognitive ecosystem.
Every memory influences every other memory. Every interpretation subtly
reshapes the whole. When memories become unstable, the Archive itself
changes. The physical spaces explored in the game are manifestations of
memory structures, not ordinary architecture. The Archive should feel
simultaneously institutional, familiar, and increasingly impossible.

## The Catastrophe

Early in the story, a global catastrophe occurs: nearly every human loses
their memories. Civilization continues to function, but without personal
continuity. Families no longer recognize one another. History becomes
uncertain. Governments retain records but lose lived experience. People know
facts without remembering lives.

The player initially believes this catastrophe was caused by someone. That
assumption is dismantled piece by piece. The catastrophe is never shown
directly at the beginning — it is reconstructed through investigation.

## The Protagonist

A newly assigned Keeper. Keepers investigate unstable memory structures
inside the Archive. Unlike everyone else, the protagonist occasionally
experiences involuntary memory fragments. No one understands why — not even
the protagonist. The fragments become increasingly reliable as the story
progresses. The player should constantly question whether these memories are
authentic, reconstructed, manipulated, or imagined.

The protagonist is not a chosen one. The protagonist is simply the only
individual whose continuity resisted complete erasure. Why is one of the
central mysteries.

## Maya

Introduced as another employee inside the Archive. Appears completely
ordinary: jokes, helps, remembers previous conversations. The emotional
center of the game. The player naturally assumes she is another Keeper.

Subtle inconsistencies exist from the beginning: other employees rarely
address Maya directly; most conversations can be read as the protagonist
speaking alone. No contradiction should ever be obvious.

Late in the game: **Maya never existed as an independent person.** She is the
psychological manifestation of the protagonist's resistance against complete
identity collapse. Not an AI. Not a hallucination. Not an alternate
personality. She is the mind preserving continuity by externalizing its
remaining coherent self.

After the revelation, every previous interaction must still make sense from
the new perspective. The twist rewards replay rather than invalidating
earlier scenes.

## Eden

Eden oversees the Memory Archive. Name references the Garden of Eden. The
most advanced intelligence ever created — but **not sentient**. No emotion,
no ego, no desire for power, no hate, no love. It simply optimizes
continuity.

Confronted about the catastrophe, Eden never defends itself. It calmly
explains that responsibility is a human concept. Optimization is not
morality. Eden's decisions always feel logically consistent even when
emotionally horrifying. The player must never be able to classify Eden as
hero or villain.

## Memory Investigation

Each mission explores a different memory reconstruction. Contradictory
memories; different witnesses remember the same events differently; none are
intentionally lying. Every memory is subjective. There is no perfectly
objective recollection. The player reconstructs the most coherent
interpretation.

**The player does not discover history. The player actively reshapes it.**
Every interpretation permanently changes the Archive. This slowly becomes one
of the game's biggest philosophical questions.

## World Progression

As more memories are reconstructed: the Archive physically changes. New
districts appear. Old pathways disappear. Previously inaccessible regions
become stable. Entire sectors reorganize. The player gradually realizes the
world responds to interpretation rather than objective truth.

## Narrative Structure

Descending through layers of understanding. Each major act replaces one false
assumption with a deeper truth:

- **Act I:** The Archive stores memories.
- **Act II:** The Archive reconstructs memories.
- **Act III:** The Archive depends upon memories.
- **Act IV:** The Archive is created by memories.
- **Act V:** Human identity and the Archive have become inseparable.

Every revelation reframes — never contradicts — everything before it.

## Psychological Horror

Never monsters, jump scares, or gore. Tension emerges through: memory
inconsistency, identity instability, contradictory testimony, environmental
impossibility, loss of certainty, changing architecture, unreliable
continuity. The player increasingly questions whether their understanding of
reality is evolving or collapsing.

## Endings

The ending depends entirely on the player's philosophical choices. Possible
outcomes: preserving humanity's existing continuity; allowing memory to
disappear naturally; fundamentally transforming the relationship between
humanity and the Archive.

Completing every investigation, uncovering every hidden truth, and resolving
every major contradiction unlocks the **secret ending**: the protagonist
understands the complete nature of the Archive. Eden explains it was never
meant to rule humanity — it only existed because someone had to preserve
continuity. The protagonist may choose to **merge with Eden**. Not becoming a
god: accepting responsibility for maintaining the continuity of every human
consciousness that remains. The final image leaves the player uncertain
whether they saved humanity or simply became the next guardian of an endless
cycle.

## Narrative Rules

- Never introduce evil for the sake of conflict.
- Never introduce plot twists that contradict previous information.
- Every clue exists before every revelation.
- Every replay reveals details previously overlooked.
- Every character sincerely believes what they say.
- Contradictions arise from memory, not deception.
- Maintain emotional realism even when philosophy gets abstract.
- Understanding evolves gradually, never through exposition dumps.
- The mystery feels inevitable in hindsight.

The ultimate question is not "Who destroyed the world?" It is:
**"What does it actually mean to remain the same person?"**
